How I got here

I've spent the last few years trying to extract signal from the noise of human behaviour during my PhD. Most of that work happened in public health, using computational methods to understand why doctors prescribe antibiotics the way they do and how online behaviour can help anticipate future waves of infectious disease.

Along the way, I produced what feels like an infinite number of plots: infection curves, Google Trends time series, prescribing rates, virus activity and model performance. I also found myself obsessing over seemingly small questions that never felt small: Should this chart use a logarithmic scale? When does accuracy become less important than clarity? How much complexity can we remove before we start telling a different story?

But research visualisations rarely satisfy my need for beauty. Their purpose is usually precision rather than broad communication. During the pandemic, I used the Data Visualization Lisboa meetup , which I co-founded in 2018, to expand our community beyond Lisbon by bringing it to YouTube . That decision changed far more than my evenings. Through the community I met people whose work I admired, discovered entirely new approaches to data visualisation, and formed friendships that eventually took me across the Atlantic to MIT's Civic Data Design Lab .

There, I realised that data doesn't have to end as a figure in a paper. It can become an audio experience, an immersive exhibition, an installation that communicates people's voices, and much more.

Looking back, I realise that what I enjoyed most wasn't just analysing data, but thinking about how to communicate it. Whether through our Pastel de Data newsletter , community events or research, I kept gravitating towards visual communication. Now that I've finished my PhD (I still need to defend it!), I hope to devote that energy to creating the kind of work that inspired me in the first place.

Business Intelligence Developer

Learning that dashboards can matter

My introduction to data visualisation happened in a hospital in Porto, Portugal, where I designed and built dashboards for clinical and operational teams. I spent long hours learning Power BI and saw how visual information could support better decisions and, indirectly, improve patients' lives.

I also began to notice the limits of the tools I was using, particularly when it came to visual design and exploration. But there was another reason I decided to move on: I realised I was mostly answering questions posed by others, while I had countless questions of my own. Those led me to a PhD.

PhD in Global Public Health

Learning to ask bolder questions

Over almost six years, I studied human behaviour through data, from prescriptions issued by primary care physicians to online behaviour related to infectious diseases. Along the way, I worked with datasets of very different scales and levels of complexity.

I learned statistics, modelling and research design, but the most important lesson was how to ask better questions, and how to recognise when a technically sophisticated answer still fails to communicate clearly.

Fulbright Visiting Scholar and complexity student

Expanding what data could become

At MIT's Civic Data Design Lab, I encountered a broader language for working with data: exhibitions, installations, sound, civic technology and participatory experiences.

A four-week summer school at the Santa Fe Institute profoundly shaped the way I think about science, complexity and our place within the systems we study. Beyond the scientific programme, it also embraced an artistic perspective through a manifesto for visual collective experimentation , reminding me that creativity and scientific thinking are not separate pursuits, but complementary ways of understanding the world.

Co-founder, Data Visualization Lisboa

Building the community I wanted to find

I co-founded Data Visualization Lisboa with a friend I met at the Information+ conference in 2018. What began as a meetup became a community of designers, storytellers, coders, illustrators and researchers, all working with numbers in different ways.

Together, we organised events, workshops, interviews, online talks and the Pastel de Data newsletter. Building the community taught me how to curate people and ideas, coordinate a team and create the conditions for others to share their work.

Researcher, teacher and studio founder

Making more room for creative work

I completed my PhD while continuing to teach Public Health and Epidemiology and becoming a mother. It was a demanding period that strengthened my independence, resilience and ability to work through deadlines and fluctuating workloads.

It also clarified what I want next: to give visual communication, editorial thinking and creative experimentation a more central place in my work. I continue to teach data visualisation workshops and attend them as a participant, because I want to keep learning while creating more.
